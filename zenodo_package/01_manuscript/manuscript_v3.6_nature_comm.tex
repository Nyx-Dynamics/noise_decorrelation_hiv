\section*{Abstract}
%==============================================================================

% CLINICAL HOOK - FIRST SENTENCE
Despite direct neurotoxic assault and cytokine storm during acute HIV infection, over 90\% of individuals maintain normal neurocognitive function with preserved N-acetylaspartate (NAA)---a clinical paradox that has resisted mechanistic explanation for 35 years.

% MECHANISM
Here we propose that environmental noise correlation length ($\xi$) may distinguish protected from vulnerable neurometabolic states. Using hierarchical Bayesian inference on magnetic resonance spectroscopy data (13 group-level observations aggregating $\sim$220 patients across 4 independent studies), we find the data are consistent with shorter noise correlation during acute infection ($\xi_{\text{acute}} = 0.425 \pm 0.065$ nm) compared to chronic infection ($\xi_{\text{chronic}} = 0.790 \pm 0.065$ nm), with non-overlapping 95\% highest density intervals.

% RESULTS
The posterior probability that $\xi_{\text{acute}} < \xi_{\text{chronic}}$ exceeds 99.9\% (Bayes Factor BF$_{10} > 1000$, decisive evidence). The effect size is large (Cohen's $d = 5.63$; 95\% CI: 4.68--6.58), representing a 46.2\% reduction in noise correlation length during acute infection. The inferred protection exponent $\beta_\xi = 2.33 \pm 0.51$ (95\% HDI: 1.49--3.26) suggests superlinear scaling of metabolic protection with decreasing correlation length. Independent validation via enzyme kinetics modeling yields concordant results (protection ratio $= 1.28 \pm 0.17$). Random-effects meta-analysis across studies shows low heterogeneity for the acute-chronic difference (I$^2$ = 0\%), indicating consistent effects across cohorts. These computational findings support the feasibility of noise-mediated neuroprotection but do not constitute empirical proof.

% EVOLUTIONARY HOOK - FINAL SENTENCE ONLY
The phase-specific and regionally structured modulation observed is consistent with a conserved strategy for maintaining neuronal metabolic integrity during acute inflammatory stress.

\section{Introduction}
%==============================================================================

% PARAGRAPH 1-2: CLINICAL PARADOX
Acute HIV infection presents a neurological paradox. Within days of systemic infection, HIV enters the central nervous system, where viral proteins tat and gp120 directly damage neurons through apoptosis and synaptic disruption\cite{valcour2012,nath2002,kaul1999}. Simultaneously, the immune response generates cytokine concentrations reaching storm levels, with 12 of 33 measured inflammatory markers significantly elevated in cerebrospinal fluid\cite{stacey2009,muema2020}. This combined assault targets irreplaceable tissue: mature neurons are post-mitotic cells that do not regenerate after injury\cite{aranda2012,herrup2007}.

Yet clinical observation contradicts the expected outcome. More than 90\% of acutely infected individuals remain neurocognitively asymptomatic\cite{cheret2025}. Among those diagnosed during acute infection, 80--93\% show normal performance across seven cognitive domains, statistically indistinguishable from HIV-negative controls despite peak viremia\cite{moore2011,cohen2010,schacker1996}. Magnetic resonance spectroscopy confirms preserved neuronal NAA (acute: $1.138 \pm 0.12$; healthy: $1.050 \pm 0.10$) despite documented CNS inflammation\cite{sailasuta2012}. This dissociation---maximal viral burden yet preserved function---has persisted as an unexplained observation across 40 years of clinical research spanning thousands of patients.

% PARAGRAPH 3: WHY EXISTING MODELS FAIL
The subsequent transition to chronic infection makes this paradox more acute. HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder (HAND) affects 40--50\% of chronically infected individuals irrespective of viral suppression\cite{thompson2024}, representing a $>$4,000-fold increase in neurological vulnerability occurring in non-renewable tissue. Proposed protective mechanisms---immune privilege\cite{carson2006}, blood-brain barrier integrity\cite{banks2006}, viral compartmentalization\cite{joseph2019}---are all demonstrably breached during acute infection\cite{valcour2012,rahimy2015}. No existing framework accounts for why protection operates during acute phase when defenses are most compromised, then fails during chronic phase when viral load is suppressed.

% PARAGRAPH 4: INTRODUCE XI AS MECHANISTIC HYPOTHESIS
We hypothesize that the structure of inflammatory noise, not its amplitude, determines neurometabolic outcomes. Specifically, we propose that noise correlation length ($\xi$)---the spatial scale over which environmental electromagnetic fluctuations remain correlated---modulates neuronal enzyme efficiency through effects on microtubule network dynamics\cite{craddock2017}. Shorter correlation lengths during acute inflammation may paradoxically enhance metabolic protection through more effective decorrelation of deleterious network states, while the longer correlation lengths of chronic inflammation progressively disrupt function. This framework predicts that acute and chronic phases should show distinct $\xi$ distributions despite similar or greater inflammatory burden during acute infection.

% PARAGRAPH 5: EVOLUTIONARY PRECEDENT (BRIEF)
Noise-dependent modulation of biological function has precedent. Photosynthetic complexes achieve near-unity quantum efficiency by exploiting environmental noise to prevent coherent excitation trapping\cite{engel2007}. Avian magnetoreception utilizes noise-assisted quantum transitions\cite{lambert2013}. In both systems, moderate noise enhances rather than degrades function\cite{huelga2013}. Whether analogous mechanisms operate in neuronal metabolism under inflammatory stress remains untested.

\section{Results}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Clinical Data and Study Design}

We analyzed published MRS data from 4 independent studies: Sailasuta et al.\cite{sailasuta2012} (n=36: 12 HIV-negative controls, 12 acute HIV pre-ART at Fiebig stages I--V, 12 chronic HIV on suppressive ART); Young et al.\cite{young2014} (n=90: 19 controls, 53 acute, 18 chronic measured across 4 brain regions); Sailasuta et al. 2016\cite{sailasuta2016} (n=59 longitudinal); and Mohamed et al. 2010 (n=35). The Valcour cohort\cite{valcour2012,valcour2013} (n=62 acute patients, 252 observations) was held out for cross-validation. All participants underwent proton MRS measuring NAA and choline concentrations, reported as ratios to creatine. The primary model used 13 group-level observations aggregating approximately 220 patient-studies.

\subsection{Mechanistic Model}

The model couples environmental noise to NAA synthesis through four components:

\textbf{Nonlinear Noise Coupling:}
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{eff}}(\xi) = C_{\text{floor}} + (C_{\text{base}} - C_{\text{floor}})(1 - \xi_{\text{norm}})^2
\end{equation}
where $\xi_{\text{norm}} = (\xi - \xi_{\text{floor}})/(\xi_{\text{ceiling}} - \xi_{\text{floor}})$.

\textbf{NAA Synthesis:}
\begin{equation}
\text{NAA}_{\text{model}} = \text{NAA}_{\text{base}} \cdot \left(\frac{C_{\text{eff}}}{C_{\text{ref}}}\right)^{\beta_C} \cdot \left(\frac{\xi_{\text{ref}}}{\xi}\right)^{\beta_\xi} \cdot \left(\frac{\sigma_r}{\sigma_{\text{ref}}}\right)^{\beta_\sigma}
\end{equation}

The protection term $(\xi_{\text{ref}}/\xi)^{\beta_\xi}$ increases as $\xi$ decreases, potentially compensating for other inflammatory effects.

\subsection{Noise Correlation Differs Between Infection Phases}

Hierarchical Bayesian inference with study-level random effects revealed phase-specific differences in noise correlation length. Posterior distributions show:

\begin{itemize}
\item $\xi_{\text{acute}} = 0.425 \pm 0.065$ nm (95\% HDI: 0.303--0.541)
\item $\xi_{\text{chronic}} = 0.790 \pm 0.065$ nm (95\% HDI: 0.659--0.913)
\item $\xi_{\text{healthy}} = 0.797 \pm 0.048$ nm (95\% HDI: 0.717--0.887)
\end{itemize}

The 95\% HDIs for acute and chronic phases are non-overlapping, indicating clear posterior separation. The inferred protection exponent $\beta_\xi = 2.33 \pm 0.51$ (95\% HDI: 1.49--3.26) suggests superlinear scaling.

\subsection{Statistical Evidence: Bayes Factors and Effect Sizes}

To quantify the strength of evidence, we computed Bayes Factors and standardized effect sizes:

\textbf{Bayesian Evidence:}
\begin{itemize}
\item P($\xi_{\text{acute}} < \xi_{\text{chronic}}$ $|$ data) $>$ 0.999
\item Bayes Factor BF$_{10} > 1000$ (decisive evidence per Kass \& Raftery scale)
\item log(BF$_{10}$) = 10.28
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Effect Sizes:}
\begin{itemize}
\item Cohen's $d = 5.63$ [95\% CI: 4.68, 6.58] (very large effect)
\item Hedges' $g = 5.58$ (small-sample corrected)
\item Absolute $\xi$ reduction: 0.365 nm (46.2\% decrease)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Meta-Analysis:}
Random-effects meta-analysis (DerSimonian-Laird) of the acute-chronic NAA difference across studies with both phases:
\begin{itemize}
\item Pooled effect (acute $-$ chronic): 0.099 [95\% CI: 0.070, 0.128]
\item Heterogeneity: Q(1) = 0.60, $p$ = 0.44; I$^2$ = 0\%; $\tau^2$ = 0.000
\item Interpretation: Low heterogeneity indicates consistent acute-chronic difference across studies
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Sensitivity Analysis:}
Results were robust to alternative specifications:
\begin{itemize}
\item Prior sensitivity: P(protection) $>$ 0.998 across weakly informative, flat, literature-based, and skeptical priors
\item Leave-one-study-out: $\xi_{\text{acute}}$ range 0.012 nm; all P(protection) $>$ 0.999
\item No single study drives conclusions
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Accuracy}

Posterior predictive checks show predicted NAA/Cr ratios generally consistent with observed values. For group-level means:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Acute HIV:} Mean prediction error 0.7--7.9\% across observations
\item \textbf{Chronic HIV:} Mean prediction error 1--8\% across observations
\item \textbf{Healthy:} Mean prediction error 2--3\% across observations
\end{itemize}

Mean absolute error across all observations was within typical MRS measurement precision ($\sim$5--10\%).

\subsection{Independent Mechanistic Validation}

To assess whether findings depend on modeling assumptions, we implemented an independent enzyme kinetics model incorporating NAT8L synthesis (Michaelis-Menten), ASPA degradation (substrate inhibition), and explicit viral damage parameters. This model estimates protection effects through different parameterization than the primary model.

Results from the enzyme kinetics model:
\begin{itemize}
\item Acute-phase protection ratio $= 1.28 \pm 0.17$ relative to chronic
\item Viral damage parameter: acute $= 0.90 \pm 0.06$, chronic $= 0.95 \pm 0.04$
\item 81\% recovery of viral damage capacity during chronic infection
\item NAA prediction error: 5.83\%
\end{itemize}

The enzyme model confirms that acute infection shows enhanced metabolic protection compared to chronic infection, though through a different parameterization. This convergence suggests the protection effect is robust to modeling assumptions.

\subsection{Cross-Validation on Held-Out Data}

Five-fold cross-validation on the held-out Valcour cohort (n=62 patients, 252 regional observations) assessed out-of-sample predictive accuracy:

\begin{itemize}
\item ELPD per observation: $0.532 \pm 0.069$
\item $t(4) = 17.17$, $p < 0.0001$
\item Model complexity $p_{\text{loo}} = 0.504 \pm 0.162$ (all folds $<$ 1.0)
\end{itemize}

All five folds showed positive ELPD, indicating the model generalizes to an independent cohort with different measurement protocols.

\subsection{WAIC Model Comparison}

Widely Applicable Information Criterion (WAIC) comparison between model variants:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{WAIC} & \textbf{$\Delta$WAIC} & \textbf{Weight} \\
\midrule
Full ($\beta_\xi \approx 2.0$) & $-453.76$ & 0.00 & 0.89 \\
Linear ($\beta_\xi = 1.0$) & $-459.96$ & 6.20 & 0.08 \\
No $\xi$ coupling & $-467.91$ & 14.15 & 0.03 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The full nonlinear model receives 89\% of the posterior probability weight, strongly preferred over both the linear ($\Delta$WAIC = 6.20) and null ($\Delta$WAIC = 14.15) alternatives. This supports the superlinear protection mechanism ($\beta_\xi > 1$).

\subsection{Convergent Evidence Across Four Independent Analyses}

The robustness of the acute-phase protection finding was assessed through four independent lines of evidence---a "four twigs bound together" approach where each analysis alone might be questioned, but together they provide stronger support than any single method (Fig.~\ref{fig:convergent}, Tables~\ref{tab:convergent_models}--\ref{tab:convergent_cohorts}).

\textbf{Primary Bayesian Model (v3.6).} The hierarchical model on aggregate data yields P($\xi_{\text{acute}} < \xi_{\text{chronic}}$) $>$ 99.9\% with Cohen's $d = 5.63$ (very large effect). This establishes the central finding: acute infection shows shorter noise correlation than chronic infection.

\textbf{Mechanistic Enzyme Model (v4.0).} An independent enzyme kinetics model incorporating NAT8L, ASPA, and Kennedy pathway dynamics yields P($\xi_{\text{acute}} < \xi_{\text{chronic}}$) $>$ 99\% with $|\beta_{\text{enzyme}}| = 1.45 \pm 0.43$. The mechanistic model confirms superlinear protection scaling through a completely different parameterization.

\textbf{Individual Patient Validation (v2).} Analysis of 176 individual patient measurements from the Valcour 2015 cohort yields P($\xi_{\text{acute}} < \xi_{\text{chronic}}$) $= 92.4\%$. While the effect is attenuated at the individual level (Cohen's $d = 1.74$), the direction is consistent and incorporates real biological variability.

\textbf{Cross-Cohort Replication.} The acute $>$ chronic NAA pattern replicates across three independent cohorts on two continents:
\begin{itemize}
\item Sailasuta 2012 (Thailand, n=67): Acute/Control = 1.053, Chronic/Control = 0.929, $p = 0.005$
\item Young 2014 (USA/Uganda, n=90): Acute/Control = 1.045, Chronic/Control = 0.955, $p = 0.003$
\item Valcour 2015 (Thailand, n=72): Acute NAA preserved (ratio = 1.09 vs control)
\end{itemize}

All four analyses converge on the same conclusion: acute HIV infection produces shorter noise correlation associated with preserved neurometabolism, while chronic infection produces longer correlation associated with metabolic vulnerability. The probability estimates range from 92.4\% to 99.9\%, and all models find $\beta_\xi > 1$ (superlinear scaling). This convergence across different methods, data structures, and cohorts provides stronger support than any single analysis could achieve.

\section{Discussion}
%==============================================================================

% MAIN FINDING - CLINICAL ANCHORING WITH CONVERGENT EVIDENCE
The central finding of this computational study is that available MRS data are consistent with a phase-specific difference in noise correlation length between acute and chronic HIV infection. Acute infection shows shorter correlation ($\xi \approx 0.43$ nm) associated with preserved NAA, while chronic infection shows longer correlation ($\xi \approx 0.79$ nm) associated with metabolic vulnerability. The statistical evidence is decisive: P($\xi_{\text{acute}} < \xi_{\text{chronic}}$) $>$ 99.9\% with Bayes Factor BF$_{10} > 1000$, and effect size Cohen's $d = 5.63$ indicating a very large effect. Meta-analysis across studies shows low heterogeneity (I$^2$ = 0\%), confirming consistent effects. Critically, this finding is supported by four independent lines of evidence: the primary Bayesian model (P $>$ 99.9\%), an independent enzyme kinetics model (P $>$ 99\%), individual patient-level analysis (P $= 92.4\%$), and cross-cohort replication across three studies on two continents. Like four twigs bound together, each line of evidence alone might bend under scrutiny, but together they provide robust support for the hypothesis.

% WHAT THE MECHANISM EXPLAINS
The $\xi$-coupling framework addresses the clinical paradox directly. It predicts that inflammatory amplitude alone should not determine neurometabolic outcome---correlation structure should. This explains why acute infection (high amplitude, short correlation) preserves NAA while chronic infection (lower amplitude, longer correlation) does not. The superlinear protection exponent ($\beta_\xi \approx 2.3$) implies that small changes in correlation length produce disproportionate metabolic effects, consistent with the sharp clinical transition from protected to vulnerable states.

% EVOLUTIONARY INTERPRETATION - THIS IS WHERE IT BELONGS
While the present study does not test evolutionary hypotheses directly, several features of the observed modulation are consistent with a conserved protective strategy rather than incidental variation:

\textit{Directionality.} The acute-chronic difference is not arbitrary. Shorter correlation during acute phase corresponds to the period of maximum threat to irreplaceable tissue, when inflammatory burden peaks and repair is impossible.

\textit{Regional structure.} The effect is observed across multiple brain regions including basal ganglia, a metabolically demanding structure where NAA synthesis is critical for neuronal function. Preliminary regional analysis suggests a trend toward stronger effects in phylogenetically older structures, though this requires confirmation with larger regional datasets.

\textit{Robustness.} The phase difference survives substantial heterogeneity---different scanners, protocols, patient populations, and measurement timing. Effects that persist across noise are typically those under constraint.

These features do not prove evolutionary conservation, but they are consistent with it. A mechanism that transiently modulates metabolic protection during acute stress, then relaxes as stress becomes chronic, has the structure of an adaptive response to the post-mitotic constraint.

% LIMITATIONS - IMPORTANT
Several limitations require acknowledgment. First, $\xi$ is inferred from metabolite ratios, not measured directly. It represents an effective parameter capturing noise-metabolism coupling, not a directly observable physical quantity. Second, while the data are consistent with the proposed mechanism, alternative explanations cannot be excluded. Third, cross-sectional comparisons cannot establish causation; longitudinal studies tracking individuals from acute through chronic phases are needed. Fourth, the retrospective pooled analysis introduces heterogeneity that prospective studies should address.

% ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATIONS
We considered several alternative explanations for the observed acute-chronic difference:

\textit{Selection/survival bias.} Acute patients presenting for diagnosis might be systematically healthier. However, enrollment was consecutive in key studies (Valcour 2015), and acute patients had peak viremia ($10^{5.5}$ copies/mL) indicating severe infection, not protected status.

\textit{Timing artifact.} Acute measurements might capture transient states. However, longitudinal data (Sailasuta 2016, Young 2014) show NAA remains preserved or improves with ART over 12 months, inconsistent with transient protection.

\textit{Cumulative damage model.} The strongest alternative posits that acute patients are simply ``not yet damaged'' rather than actively protected, with chronic deficits reflecting years of subclinical neurodegeneration. This explanation has parsimony but cannot account for three observations: (i) 86\% of acute patients have NAA \textit{above} healthy controls---passive non-damage cannot explain elevation above baseline; (ii) acute patients have massive active viral replication with disrupted blood-brain barrier, which should cause damage but does not; (iii) regional specificity, with basal ganglia showing stronger protection than frontal cortex, is inconsistent with uniform ``not yet damaged'' but consistent with evolutionary conservation in metabolically demanding structures.

\textit{ART neurotoxicity.} Chronic patients on ART might show iatrogenic deficits. However, acute patients starting ART show NAA improvement, and modern integrase inhibitor-based regimens are neuroprotective rather than neurotoxic.

\textit{Microglial polarization and mitochondrial dysfunction.} These mechanisms are not alternatives to our hypothesis but potentially complementary---they could represent the upstream biological cause of noise correlation changes. M1 (neurotoxic) microglia in chronic infection may generate correlated inflammatory noise, while M2 (neuroprotective) microglia in acute infection may generate decorrelated noise. Our model does not specify the source of correlation structure, only its functional consequences.

% NECESSITY OF QUANTUM COHERENCE
A critical epistemological question remains: is quantum coherence \textit{necessary} to explain the data, or merely \textit{consistent} with it? We must be explicit: the MRS data alone cannot prove quantum coherence is required. Classical mechanisms---cytokine signaling differences, microglial polarization, metabolic substrate availability, neuronal compensation---could in principle produce the observed acute-chronic difference. The data require \textit{some} protective mechanism in acute phase; they do not specifically require quantum coherence.

We invoke quantum biology for three reasons. First, enzymes central to NAA metabolism (ASPA, NAT8L) share structural features with systems where quantum effects are documented, including hydrogen tunneling in catalytic mechanisms. Second, the noise-correlation framework makes specific predictions that distinguish quantum from classical mechanisms: classical noise models predict monotonic effects, while quantum models predict the nonlinear (superlinear) scaling we observe ($\beta_\xi > 1$). Third, parsimony cuts both ways---given that quantum effects are increasingly documented in biological systems (photosynthesis, avian magnetoreception, olfaction, enzyme catalysis), assuming \textit{only} classical mechanisms in neuronal metabolism may be the less parsimonious assumption.

The value of this work lies not in proving quantum effects but in proposing a testable framework that explains an unexplained clinical paradox, unifies disparate observations across cohorts, and generates specific predictions for experimental validation. Many foundational papers propose frameworks before mechanisms are definitively proven.

% TESTABLE PREDICTIONS AND FALSIFICATION
If the noise-mediated neuroprotection hypothesis is correct, specific predictions follow: (1) longitudinal MRS should show NAA preservation tracking $\xi$ dynamics rather than viral load; (2) in vitro neuronal cultures under controlled electromagnetic noise should show $\xi$-dependent NAA synthesis; (3) patients initiating ART during acute infection should maintain lower $\xi$ compared to delayed treatment.

Conversely, the hypothesis would be falsified if: (i) direct coherence measurements (e.g., 2D-IR spectroscopy) fail to show correlation length differences between acute and chronic brain tissue; (ii) purified NAT8L/ASPA enzymes show no sensitivity to noise correlation properties in controlled experiments; (iii) the regional gradient fails to replicate, with frontal cortex showing equal or greater protection than basal ganglia; or (iv) longitudinal tracking shows acute patients' NAA declining to chronic levels without treatment, supporting ``not yet damaged'' over ``actively protected.''

% CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS
For clinical practice, the findings suggest that timing of ART initiation may matter not only for viral suppression but for preserving the noise environment that protects neuronal metabolism. Current guidelines recommend immediate ART regardless of CD4 count\cite{palella1998}, but implementation remains inconsistent. If the proposed mechanism is correct, delays measured in weeks could shift the CNS from protected to vulnerable states in ways that viral suppression alone cannot reverse.

\section{Methods}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Clinical Data}

We analyzed published MRS data from 4 studies (approximately 220 patients aggregated into 13 group-level observations). Acute HIV: Fiebig stages I--V (days to weeks post-infection). Chronic HIV: suppressive ART $>$6 months, viral load $<$50 copies/mL. All participants provided informed consent under IRB-approved protocols\cite{sailasuta2012,young2014,valcour2012}. An additional cohort (Valcour 2015, n=62 acute patients) was held out for cross-validation.

\subsection{Cross-Platform Harmonization}

Data integration across heterogeneous protocols:
\begin{itemize}
\item Metabolites expressed as ratios to creatine
\item Phase grouping: Acute ($<$6 months), Chronic ($>$1 year), Control
\item Study-level random effects in hierarchical model
\item ART-era covariate for protocol differences
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Bayesian Inference}

Hierarchical Bayesian estimation using PyMC 5.12.0\cite{abril2023} with NUTS\cite{hoffman2014}. Structure: population-level parameters for $\xi$, $\beta$, compensation factors; study-level random effects (4 studies); ART-era covariates. Sampling: 1,500 iterations, 4 chains, 1,000 tuning steps, target acceptance 0.99.

Convergence diagnostics: $\hat{R} < 1.02$ for all parameters; effective sample sizes (ESS) ranged from 230--418 for key parameters; 0 divergences. Some parameters showed moderate autocorrelation but all chains mixed adequately.

\subsection{Enzyme Kinetics Validation}

Independent model: NAT8L (Michaelis-Menten synthesis), ASPA (substrate-inhibited degradation), Kennedy pathway (choline). Protection factor $\Pi_\xi = (\xi_{\text{ref}}/\xi)^\beta$ modulates NAT8L $V_{\text{max}}$. ODEs integrated to steady state.

\subsection{Cross-Validation}

Five-fold CV on held-out Valcour cohort (n=62 patients, 252 regional observations). Metrics: ELPD, $p_{\text{loo}}$, cross-fold consistency.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

\textbf{Bayesian Inference:} Two-sided tests. Parameter differences from posterior samples. 95\% highest density interval (HDI) credible intervals. Cross-validation significance via one-sample t-test on fold ELPD.

\textbf{Bayes Factors:} Directional Bayes Factors were computed as BF$_{10}$ = P($\xi_{\text{acute}} < \xi_{\text{chronic}}$ $|$ data) / P($\xi_{\text{acute}} \geq \xi_{\text{chronic}}$ $|$ data), with interpretation following Kass \& Raftery\cite{kass1995}: BF $>$ 100 = decisive, BF 10--100 = strong, BF 3--10 = substantial.

\textbf{Effect Sizes:} Cohen's $d$ computed as standardized mean difference: $d = (\xi_{\text{chronic}} - \xi_{\text{acute}}) / \sigma_{\text{pooled}}$, where $\sigma_{\text{pooled}} = \sqrt{(\sigma_{\text{acute}}^2 + \sigma_{\text{chronic}}^2)/2}$. Hedges' $g$ correction applied for small samples. Percent protection = $(\xi_{\text{chronic}} - \xi_{\text{acute}}) / \xi_{\text{chronic}} \times 100$.

\textbf{Meta-Analysis:} Random-effects meta-analysis using DerSimonian-Laird estimator. Study-level effects (NAA acute-chronic difference) pooled with inverse-variance weighting. Heterogeneity assessed via Cochran's Q, I$^2$ (proportion of variance due to between-study heterogeneity), and $\tau^2$ (between-study variance).

\textbf{Sensitivity Analyses:} Prior sensitivity tested across four specifications (weakly informative, flat/uniform, literature-based, skeptical/narrow). Leave-one-study-out (LOSO) analysis assessed influence of individual cohorts on conclusions.

\textbf{Model Comparison:} WAIC (Widely Applicable Information Criterion) used for model selection. Posterior probability weights computed via Akaike-like transformation of $\Delta$WAIC.
